Best deal management tool without a CRM in 2026

You have deals in progress. You need to know what stage they're in, when to follow up, and which ones are going cold. The obvious answer is a CRM — but you've tried that, or you already know you won't maintain one.

The good news: you can track deals without a CRM. Here's how people actually do it, and which approach works best.

What deal management actually requires

Strip away CRM jargon and you need four things:

  1. Know what stage each deal is in — lead, contacted, proposal sent, negotiation, won/lost
  2. Know the next action and when — "follow up with Sarah on Thursday"
  3. Catch stale deals — which conversations have gone silent without you noticing
  4. See the full picture — how many active deals, total pipeline value, what needs attention today

A CRM does all of this — plus 500 other things you don't need. These tools do just the essentials.

Tool-by-tool breakdown

NeverMiss

A Chrome extension that adds deal context to Gmail. Tag threads with a stage and value, set follow-up dates, and see a pipeline summary — all without leaving your inbox. Stale deals get nudged automatically.

  • Strengths: Zero context-switching, automatic stale alerts, open tracking, daily follow-up queue
  • Limitations: Gmail-only, not a standalone database, newer product
  • Price: Free forever (with limits) or $12.99/month

Google Sheets

The classic DIY approach. Create columns for deal name, contact, stage, value, last contact date, and next follow-up. Sort by next follow-up date each morning.

  • Strengths: Free, infinitely customizable, familiar interface
  • Limitations: Entirely manual, no email integration, drifts from reality within days, no stale deal detection, no open tracking
  • Price: Free

Notion

Build a deal database with custom properties, board views for pipeline stages, and formulas to calculate days since last contact. Powerful if you enjoy building systems.

  • Strengths: Beautiful, flexible, good board view, can connect to other workflows
  • Limitations: No email integration, requires manual updates, no stale alerts, time-consuming to maintain, becomes another app to check
  • Price: Free or $10/month

Trello

Create a board with columns for each pipeline stage. Each card is a deal. Drag cards as deals progress. Add due dates for follow-up reminders.

  • Strengths: Visual, intuitive drag-and-drop, simple to set up
  • Limitations: Cards go stale without anyone noticing, no email integration, limited deal metadata, becomes a graveyard over time
  • Price: Free or $5/month

Airtable

A spreadsheet-database hybrid with custom views, automations, and integrations. More powerful than Sheets, more structured than Notion.

  • Strengths: Powerful automations, multiple views (grid, kanban, calendar), API access
  • Limitations: Expensive at scale, steep learning curve, still requires manual email logging, another separate app
  • Price: Free or $20/month

Bottom line

If you like building systems and don't mind manual updates, Notion or Airtable give you the most flexibility. If you want something free and simple, a spreadsheet works until it doesn't.
