How to follow up after a cold email

You sent a cold email to a prospect you've never spoken to. They didn't reply. Now what?

Most cold emails don't get a reply on the first send — that's normal, not a failure. The difference between successful cold outreach and wasted effort is almost always the follow-up.

When to send the first follow-up

2–3 business days after your initial email. This is soon enough that they still remember (or at least recognize) your name, but not so soon that it feels aggressive.

If you have email open tracking, use it: if they opened your first email but didn't reply, they saw it and chose not to act. Your follow-up should add a new angle, not just repeat the same ask.

A simple cold email follow-up cadence

Email 1 (Day 0): The initial outreach

Short, relevant, personalized. One clear ask.

Email 2 (Day 2–3): The gentle bump

Reply to your own thread. Keep it to 2–3 sentences. Reference the original email and add one small piece of value — a relevant stat, a case study, or a clarifying question.

"Hi Alex — following up on my note from Tuesday. Thought this might be relevant: [brief value-add]. Worth a 10-minute call this week?"

Email 3 (Day 6–7): The different angle

If they haven't responded to two emails, don't send a third one saying the same thing. Try a different angle — a different benefit, a question about their current process, or social proof.

"Hi Alex — curious: how are you currently handling follow-ups with prospects who go quiet? We helped [similar company] recover 30% of stale deals with a simple system."

Email 4 (Day 14): The value-first check-in

Lead with something useful — an article, a resource, an insight relevant to their industry. The ask is secondary.

Email 5 (Day 21–28): The breakup

Be honest. Let them know you're closing the loop. This often triggers replies from people who were "meaning to get back to you."

"Hi Alex — I haven't heard back, so I'll assume the timing isn't right. No hard feelings. If things change, I'm here."

Rules for cold follow-ups

  • Always reply to your own thread. Don't start a new email — it loses the context and feels disconnected.
  • Each follow-up should add something new. If you're just saying "checking in," you're wasting their time and yours.
  • Keep it short. Cold follow-ups should be 2–4 sentences. Respect their time.
  • Don't apologize for following up. "Sorry to bother you" signals that you think your email isn't worth reading.
  • Use open data to adapt. Someone who opened your email 5 times is interested but hesitating — that changes your approach.

The real problem: remembering to follow up

Most people know they should follow up. The problem is they forget, or they're too busy, or the thread gets buried. A follow-up cadence only works if you actually execute it.
