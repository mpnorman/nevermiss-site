How to track email opens in Gmail

Gmail doesn't tell you when someone opens your email. You send a proposal, a follow-up, a quote — and then you wait, wondering if they even saw it. Did it land in spam? Did they read it and lose interest? Are they about to reply?

Email open tracking solves this. Here's how it works, what tools add it to Gmail, and — more importantly — how to actually use open data to close more deals.

How email open tracking works

When you install a tracking extension, it inserts a tiny invisible image (called a tracking pixel) into your outgoing emails. When the recipient opens the email and their email client loads images, the pixel pings a server — recording that the email was opened, when, and roughly how many times.

Limitations to know about:

Open tracking isn't perfect. Some email clients block images by default (so the open won't register). Apple Mail's privacy features can pre-load images, which may inflate open counts. Treat open data as a useful signal, not a guarantee.

Can you track opens natively in Gmail?

Gmail offers read receipts, but only for Google Workspace accounts — and only when the recipient's admin has enabled them. The recipient also gets to choose whether to send the receipt. For practical sales use, this isn't reliable.

To get real open tracking in Gmail, you need a Chrome extension.

Open tracking alone isn't enough

Here's where most people stop: they install a tracking tool, see green checkmarks, and feel informed. But knowing someone opened your email isn't the same as acting on it.

The real value of open data is in what you do with it:

  • Prioritize your follow-ups. A contact who opened your email 3 times today is warmer than one who hasn't opened it at all. Your follow-up queue should reflect this.
  • Time your follow-up. If someone consistently opens your emails at 7:30am, that's when to send. Open patterns reveal the best send window for each contact.
  • Spot the stale deals. If your email has been sitting unopened for 5 days, the deal is going cold. You need to know this before it's too late to recover.
  • Decide when to stop. If a contact has opened your last 3 emails without replying, the problem isn't visibility — it's interest. That changes your approach.
