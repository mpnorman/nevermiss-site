How to keep track of deals without a CRM

You know you should track your deals somewhere. You've probably tried a CRM, or at least thought about it. But CRMs are built for sales teams with admins, and you're one person (or a small team) who just needs to know: what's in play, what stage is it in, and what do I need to do next?

Here are the three most practical approaches, from simplest to most automated.

Option 1: The spreadsheet

How it works

Create a Google Sheet with columns: Deal Name, Contact, Stage, Value, Last Contact Date, Next Follow-Up, Notes. Sort by Next Follow-Up each morning.

Pros: Free, flexible, familiar. You can customize it however you want.

Cons: Entirely manual. You have to update it after every email, every call, every stage change. Within a week, reality drifts from the spreadsheet. Within a month, it's abandoned.

Option 2: A project board (Notion, Trello)

How it works

Create a board with columns for each pipeline stage. Each card is a deal. Drag cards between stages as deals progress. Add due dates for follow-up reminders.

Pros: Visual, intuitive, satisfying to drag cards across.

Cons: Still manual. No connection to your email — you have to switch tabs to update. Cards go stale without anyone noticing. Becomes a graveyard over time.

Option 3: A tool that lives in your inbox

How it works

Install a Chrome extension that adds deal tracking directly to Gmail. Tag threads with stages and values. Set follow-up dates at the moment you send. Get a daily queue of what needs attention.

Pros: Zero context-switching. Updates happen where the conversations happen. Stale deals get caught automatically. Open tracking shows who's engaged.

Cons: Gmail-only. Less customizable than a spreadsheet or Notion.

Which one sticks?

The best system is the one you'll actually use. Spreadsheets and boards work in theory but fail in practice because they require a separate behavior: after every email, go update the tracker. That behavior dies on your first busy day.

Inbox-based tools stick because the tracking happens at the moment of action — you send an email, you set the follow-up. No extra step, no separate tab, no discipline required beyond the initial click.
